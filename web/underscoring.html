Structural Underscoring in Sleep Medicine

How AASM Scoring Rules and CMS Reimbursement Policy Systematically Reduce Reported Sleep Apnea Severity

1. The AASM ↔ CMS Influence Loop

The American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM) writes the clinical scoring rules that define how sleep studies are interpreted. The Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) references these rules in coverage determinations — but selectively adopts a stricter variant that reduces diagnostic sensitivity. The result is a feedback loop where clinical science and cost containment are structurally at odds.

How AASM influences CMS

  • RUC (Relative Value Scale Update Committee): AASM representatives sit on the AMA's RUC, which recommends RVUs for sleep CPT codes. CMS accepts RUC recommendations >90% of the time. However, AASM has acknowledged they lack representation in the AMA House of Delegates, which weakens their influence over the RUC process.1
  • Clinical practice guidelines: AASM writes the scoring manual (AASM Manual for the Scoring of Sleep and Associated Events) which defines how PSG and HSAT are interpreted. CMS and MACs reference these guidelines in Local Coverage Determinations (LCDs).
  • Comment letters: AASM files formal comments on every annual MPFS (Medicare Physician Fee Schedule) proposed rule. These are public on regulations.gov.
  • JCSM editorials: Position papers in the Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine become the intellectual basis for LCD changes.

How CMS influences clinical behavior

  • Reimbursement rates determine whether sleep labs are profitable. CMS cuts to PSG rates have driven the shift toward HSAT.
  • Coverage determinations (LCDs/NCDs) define which patients qualify for PAP coverage — and which scoring rule must be used.
  • The 90-day PAP compliance rule (CFR §410.38(c)) forces clinics to document adherence at 30/60/90 days or Medicare stops paying for the device.
  • Medicare Advantage plans layer additional prior authorization requirements that further restrict access.

2. Three Structural Underscoring Mechanisms

Mechanism A: The Hypopnea Scoring War (AASM 1A vs CMS 1B)

This is the single most important political fact in sleep medicine.

RuleDefinitionEffect on AHIWho uses it
AASM Rule 1A (recommended)Hypopnea = ≥30% airflow reduction for ≥10 sec with ≥3% O₂ desat OR arousalHigher AHI — more events countedAASM-accredited centers (clinical reporting)
CMS Rule 1B (mandated for Medicare)Hypopnea = ≥30% airflow reduction for ≥10 sec with ≥4% O₂ desat onlyLower AHI — arousals excluded, higher thresholdAll Medicare claims; many commercial payers

CMS chose 1B as a cost-containment strategy. Fewer patients cross the AHI ≥5 threshold → fewer OSA diagnoses → fewer PAP prescriptions → lower Medicare spend.

Impact: 26.4% of patients who have OSA under 1A scoring are reclassified as NOT having OSA (or less severe) under 1B scoring.2

Mechanism B: HSAT vs In-Lab PSG (Mechanical Underscoring)

Home Sleep Apnea Testing (HSAT) mechanically underscores AHI because:

  • No EEG → cannot measure actual sleep time → uses total recording time (TRT) as denominator → dilutes AHI
  • Patient awake during recording → time awake inflates denominator → AHI drops
  • Fewer channels → misses central apneas, upper airway resistance events, some hypopneas
  • Age bias: older patients spend more time awake → greater underestimation5

Published finding: of all patients with OSA on PSG, 26.4% would be reclassified as having less severe or no OSA after recalculating AHI using time-in-bed (HSAT methodology) rather than total sleep time (PSG methodology).3

Mechanism C: Combined Stack (HSAT + 1B Scoring)

When a practice uses HSAT (mechanical underscoring) and scores with CMS 1B rule (regulatory underscoring), the two effects compound. A patient whose true in-lab 1A AHI is 18 (moderate OSA) could score as AHI 9 (mild) or even AHI 4 (no OSA) under HSAT + 1B. This is not fraud — it is the structural result of CMS cost-containment policy applied to a less-sensitive diagnostic modality.
